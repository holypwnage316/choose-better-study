Overview

The Law Model focuses on obeying God's commands as revealed in Scripture, particularly the moral law summarized in the Ten Commandments.


Has God, in Holy Scripture, commanded or prohibited this behavior?

God's Authority to Rule

God has the rightful authority to direct that which he has made. This model acknowledges:


1) He has the right to govern us

2) He has the perfect wisdom and love by which to govern us

Scripture on God's Authority

Psalm 100:3

Know that the Lord, he is God! It is he who made us, and we are his; we are his people, and the sheep of his pasture.

1 Corinthians 6:20

You were bought with a price. So glorify God in your body.

We were created by God, sustained by him, and redeemed by him — thrice obligated to serve him.

Discussion Question

How does the secular world "borrow capital" from the biblical understanding of the law model?

Biblical Basis: Genesis

God not only creates, but in the very act, issues a decree for how each aspect of what he has created is to serve him.


God effectively commanded each aspect of the created order to serve him by fulfilling a particular task — including the human task to "have dominion" over the earth.


All human suffering results from Adam's disobedience of God's prohibition against eating the fruit of one particular tree.

Biblical Basis: Sinai to the Great Commission

Mount Sinai

The covenant at Sinai through Moses is characterized by laws; Moses brings down "ten words" from the mountain — at least nine of them are commands.

The Great Commission

After his resurrection, Christ instructs his apostles to teach others "to observe all that I have commanded you."


From Genesis to the Great Commission, God's commands are woven throughout the whole story of Scripture.

The Law Model & Human Nature

The law model has played a dominant role in the church's discussion of ethics, especially since the Protestant Reformation.


  • Luther's Small Catechism
  • The Heidelberg Catechism
  • The Westminster Shorter & Larger Catechisms

All contain an exposition of the decalogue (Ten Commandments).

Benefits of the Law Model

Promotes Humility

We recognize that we exist under the authority of our Maker.

Provides Specific Direction

You shall not murder. You shall not commit adultery. You shall not steal. You shall not covet.


The human race is not left alone to discover every ethical answer by trial and error.

Law & Our Fallen Nature

The law model is necessary because our affections and desires have been tainted by sin.


Like a child who reaches up to touch a hot stove, we often exhibit an unhealthy curiosity to explore dangerous areas.


Romans 5:13

Sin indeed was in the world before the law was given.

Galatians 3:19

Why then the law? It was added because of transgressions.

Scripture Reading

Let's have someone read 1 Timothy 1:8-10


Some commentators think Paul is being hyperbolic here, but David Gordon argues we have every reason to take his statement at face value.


Biblical commands are a kind gift from God for us whose moral nature is fallen, just as a parent's warning to their child about not touching a hot stove.

Discussion Question

Rightly understood, the law model should foster humility rather than pride and legalism. Why?

How the Law Model Functions

As we consider any behavioral choice, the model asks:

Does God command or prohibit this behavior?

  • Is it okay to desire another person's possessions? → God prohibits covetousness
  • Desiring to injure someone in rage? → God prohibits murder and malicious thoughts
  • What is our duty to our parents? → God commands us to honor them

This directness and clarity is what makes the law model such an efficient model for ethics.

Discussion Question

How does interaction with the other models prevent the law model from becoming a tool for promoting legalism?

Special Challenges

Some commands in Scripture do not oblige everyone. We must learn to distinguish:


  1. Local vs. Universal Laws
  2. Temporary vs. Eternal Laws
  3. Covenant Stipulations vs. Moral Principles

Local vs. Universal Laws

Some commands are addressed to specific individuals or groups.


2 Timothy 4:13

"When you come, bring the cloak that I left with Carpus at Troas, also the books, and above all the parchments."

Evidently, this command only applies to Timothy.

Local vs. Universal Laws

However, a few verses earlier:

2 Timothy 4:5

"As for you, always be sober-minded, endure suffering, do the work of an evangelist, fulfill your ministry."


David Gordon recommends asking a twofold question:

  • To whom was the command originally given?
  • Is my relation to God the same as that of the original recipient?

Temporary vs. Eternal Laws

Some laws are temporary obligations rather than obligations for all people for all time.


Many Mosaic laws obliged old covenant Israelites but do not oblige new covenant believers:

  • Circumcision
  • Ceremonial laws
  • Civil laws

The Jerusalem Council in Acts 15 reveals the apostles themselves did not have an immediate answer to this problem.

The "Forever" Problem

Some commands are described as "eternal" or "forever" even though they no longer apply.


Leviticus 23:14

"A statute forever throughout your generations"


The Feast of Firstfruits was called "forever" — yet the Jewish calendar expired with the resurrection of Christ.


We must distinguish commands that delineate the relation between creatures and their Creator from those that merely lasted for the duration of a given covenant administration.

Discussion Question

Can you think of an Old Testament command that was described as "eternal" or "forever" but no longer applies to new covenant believers?

How do you know it no longer applies?

Covenant Stipulations vs. Moral Principles

All biblical laws are in fact covenant stipulations, placed by God on the parties with whom he covenants.


Just as you are not obligated to make mortgage payments to my banker, nor I to yours — the commands in the Bible are addressed to, and oblige only, a specific covenant people.

Seven Guidelines

How do we determine if a command obliges us also?

  1. Is the law reiterated in another covenant administration?
  2. Is the law reiterated in the covenant in which we find ourselves?
  3. Is the law supplied prior to the fall?
  4. Does the command describe the final, redeemed state?
  5. Is the law supplied with an extra-covenantal rationale?
  6. Does the command reflect a moral attribute of God?
  7. Does the command reflect the distinctive features of a given covenant administration?

Discussion Question

What does it mean to see "covenantal integrity" between the stipulations of the Sinai covenant and those of the new covenant?

Summary

Every biblical law is governed by two things:


God's Unchanging Moral Character

The Creator's innate, morally praiseworthy, and unchanging moral character

God's Purposes for Particular Creatures

His varying purposes for various creatures and covenant communities


The law requiring circumcision, and the apostolic mandate refusing its requirement, are both consistent with God's moral character — but reflect different purposes for different covenant communities.

Key Takeaways

2. The moral law reflects God's character
