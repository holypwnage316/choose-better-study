Overview

This study introduces Choose Better by David Gordon and its central premise:


Scripture provides not one but five distinct models for making ethical decisions.


Each model has its place, and mature believers learn when and how to apply each one.

What is Ethics?

Ethics is the study of how to live and how to live well.


Within a theistic viewpoint, ethics can be seen as trying to live out our lives as God has intended us to live.


"Every decision we make either contributes to or detracts from our human life as God created it."

Human Nature & Decision Making

Thinking is a reflection of human nature.

  • Other creatures run mostly on instinct
  • We have the power to reason, think, and assess before deciding
  • We can consider short-term and long-term effects
  • We can weigh pros and cons

Socrates: "The unexamined life is not worth living for a human."

Real-Life Decisions

Throughout life, we face decisions with lasting impacts:

  • Should aging parents live with us or in a care facility?
  • Should we permit our child to train for gymnastics every weekday evening?
  • How do we handle a disobedient child? What about persistent disobedience?

"Ethics is not merely about answering choice-questions correctly; it's also about asking them in the first place." — David Gordon

Good vs. Better

Most of the time we are not choosing between good and evil.


We are most often choosing between an okay or good choice vs. a better choice.


Our ethical decision making is not merely to distinguish good from bad, but more often, to distinguish good from better.

Scripture Reading

Luke 10:38-42

Now as they went on their way, Jesus entered a village. And a woman named Martha welcomed him into her house. And she had a sister called Mary, who sat at the Lord's feet and listened to his teaching. But Martha was distracted with much serving...

Jesus said to her, "Martha, Martha, you are anxious and troubled about many things, but one thing is necessary. Mary has chosen the good portion, which will not be taken away from her."

Mary's Better Choice

Serving others is not wrong — in fact, it's a good thing to do.


However, at this particular time, Mary had made the better choice.


By listening to Jesus's instruction, she had "chosen the good portion."


One choice was good. Another choice was better.

The Five Models

David Gordon identifies five models for ethical decision-making in Scripture.


"Differing traditions 'model' the ethical enterprise differently, and the combination of these five models yields more fruit than any one by itself would."


Think of each model as a tool in a toolbox — different tools for different jobs, all working toward the same goal.

The Five Models


  1. Imitation
  2. Law
  3. Wisdom
  4. Communion
  5. Warfare

Each model asks a different set of questions and brings its own insight.

Model Summaries (1-2)

1. The Imitation Model

Focus: Being like God on a creaturely scale — what one is vs. what one does

Key Question: Does this decision allow me to emulate God or cultivate traits that reflect his image?

2. The Law Model

Focus: Obeying God who has rightful authority to order his creation

Key Question: Has God, in Holy Scripture, commanded or prohibited this behavior?

Model Summaries (3-4)

3. The Wisdom Model

Focus: Making wise choices moment by moment; observing and learning from outcomes

Key Question: What is the likely outcome of my doing or not doing this?

4. The Communion Model

Focus: Life as communication and fellowship with God; life itself is prayer

Key Question: How might this option enhance or hinder my communion with God?

Model Summary (5)

5. The Warfare Model

Focus: Life as a war where we are enlisted soldiers; duties are both defensive and offensive

Key Question: In the often invisible yet real warfare between the forces of good and evil, will this decision likely serve the forces of good or evil?


Each model is necessary, yet insufficient by itself, to inform every ethical quandary.

Key Takeaways

1. Ethics is not one-size-fits-all

2. Scripture models multiple approaches to moral reasoning

3. Wisdom involves knowing which model to apply in which situation
