Overview

The Imitation Model calls us to follow the example of Christ and other godly individuals in Scripture.


We look at how Jesus lived, how Paul conducted his ministry, and how the heroes of faith responded to challenges.


Does this decision allow us to emulate God or to cultivate human traits that reflect his image?

A Foundational Model


"From the earliest days of the Christian church to the present... from the most sophisticated Christian theologian to the simplest disciple... the imitation model has captivated those who have reflected upon it."


Entire generations of the church have spoken the language of the imitation model to express what it means to live as a Christian.

The Biblical Basis

The imitation model arises naturally from twin realities:


1) Humans alone were created in the image of God

2) We are commanded to be like God

Created in His Image

Genesis 1:26-27

Then God said, "Let us make man in our image, after our likeness. And let them have dominion over the fish of the sea and over the birds of the heavens and over the livestock and over all the earth and over every creeping thing that creeps on the earth."

So God created man in his own image, in the image of God he created him; male and female he created them.

Four times in two verses, God states that humans are to be made like him.

Commanded to Be Like God

Let's read some examples through Scripture:


  • Leviticus 19:2
  • John 13:34
  • Romans 8:29
  • Ephesians 5:1
  • 1 John 3:2

We are called to be like God and imitate him.

The Imitation Model & Human Nature

Humans learn by imitation — it's one of the most fundamental aspects of our created nature.


Why does an American child speak English? Why does a French child speak French?


We make hundreds, maybe thousands, of behavioral choices simply by imitating what we observe around us.

Virtue or Vice

Before the fall, our capacity for imitation was purely a blessing — we were only imitating God himself and innocent humans.


But after the fall, we either imitate virtue or vice.


3 John 1:11

"Beloved, do not imitate evil but imitate good. Whoever does good is from God; whoever does evil has not seen God."

Imago Dei

The capacity to imitate was one of the great gifts God gave us.


Implicit in our being made in God's image is being uniquely endowed with the capacity to behave like God on a creaturely scale.


Had we remained innocent in the garden, imitation alone would have been sufficient for the rest of our lives.

Imitating God's Traits & Deeds

The imitation model teaches us to imitate God's traits (attributes) and his deeds (works).


Some of God's attributes cannot be imitated — the incommunicable attributes:

  • God is immutable; we are not
  • God is eternal; we are not
  • God is infinite; we are not

What We Can Imitate

Even amongst incommunicable acts, there is something we can imitate.


God created out of nothing — we create out of what already exists and fashion it for a new purpose.


We cannot atone for one another's sins, but we can speak truthful and loving words that direct others to the Christian gospel.


We can also "cover" the imperfections of others by keeping them private (9th commandment; WLC Q&A 143-145).

Making, Sustaining, Redeeming

Broadly speaking, God's works are of:


Making — humans who are creative are imitating God

Sustaining — good stewards of resources are imitating God

Redeeming — those who heal wounds, fix injuries, or reconcile strife are imitating God

God's Deeds are Four

All that God has done falls into one of four categories:


  1. God creates
  2. God judges
  3. God sustains
  4. God redeems

At each moment we ask: is there an opportunity to be like our making, judging, preserving, and repairing God?

Seven Questions for Each Trait

  1. What is the trait?
  2. What Scriptures teach that God has this trait?
  3. Why is this trait praiseworthy?
  4. Why is it important to us, as creatures, that God has this trait?
  5. Why is it important to us, as fallen creatures, that God has this trait?
  6. What can we do, with the Holy Spirit's aid, to cultivate this trait?
  7. What attitudes or behaviors prohibit cultivating this trait?

Worked Example: Justice

Let's walk through the seven questions using justice as our example.


What is justice?

"The attainment of what is just, especially that which is fair, moral, right, merited, or in accordance with law"


  • Perfection of moral character that abhors wickedness and loves uprightness
  • Equity or impartiality — people treated with regard for what they have actually done

Justice in Scripture

Psalm 11:7

"For the Lord is righteous; he loves righteous deeds; the upright shall behold his face."

1 Peter 1:17

"If you call on him as Father who judges impartially according to each one's deeds, conduct yourselves with fear throughout the time of your exile."

Why Is Justice Praiseworthy?

For many, this trait is not immediately attractive — as sinners, we may fear God's justice.


God's justice is perhaps best appreciated when we witness its absence.


  • When we are judged unjustly — whether civil, ecclesiastical, or domestic
  • When the wicked are not held accountable
  • When judges are bribed

We instinctively recoil — we appreciate justice by abhorring injustice.

Why Does God's Justice Matter?

As creatures

God assures us he is not blind to injustice. The wicked may get away with things in this life but not forever. At the return of Christ, there will be an accounting for every thought, word, and deed.

As fallen creatures

We may initially fear God's justice; however, as we consider Christ's perfect obedience, we understand how fully he satisfied divine justice.

Cultivating Justice

We can ask ourselves whether we are being just in the offices of power we hold:


  • Husbands — are you just in leading your wife?
  • Parents — are you shepherding your kids justly?
  • Supervisors — are you fair with those under you?
  • Teachers — do you grade impartially?

What Prohibits Justice?

False Sympathy

Sympathy is appropriate for pain and heartache, but not when evaluating whether someone has done right or wrong.

Fearing Men More Than God

When we are more concerned about pleasing people than God, we pervert justice.

Partiality

We cannot permit our judgements to be influenced by personal considerations. We must evaluate with consistent, impartial standards.

Special Challenges

The imitation model comes with challenges:


It requires deep study

Compared to the law model, the imitation model is not fast. We need to know and understand God's traits and works before we can imitate them.

Not all traits can be imitated

The incommunicable attributes — eternity, omniscience, omnipotence — are attributes God alone has and does not share with his creatures.


These limitations are why David Gordon presents five models rather than just one.
