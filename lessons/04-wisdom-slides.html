The Wisdom Model applies prudence and discernment to navigate complex situations where simple commands may not directly apply.


Life presents countless decisions where no explicit command exists — and wisdom helps us choose the better path.

Wisdom as Complement


Wisdom is a necessary complement to the law (and the other models) if one is to live well.


Wisdom is the capacity to observe the true nature of things, and therefore to understand what works and what does not.


Example: There are no biblical commands about digital technologies. Does that mean every decision about their use is equally wise, productive, or humane?

The Biblical Basis

The wisdom model arises from Scripture's teaching about our ability to discover the latent capacities and potentials of God's created order.


1 Corinthians 6:12

"All things are lawful for me, but not all things are helpful."


Those who fail to observe and evaluate are called fools. Wisdom invites us to learn, ask questions, and reflect — what Gordon calls "the contemplative life."

Wisdom & Human Nature

Humans are made in the image of a wise God who, by wisdom, created all things.


  • We have the ability to observe and learn from what we observe
  • Our choices have consequences — some accord with God's will, some do not
  • Some contribute to our health; others do not
  • Some align with the natural order; some do not

Beyond Pragmatism

The wisdom model considers more than behavioral consequences — it considers the natural order itself.


Proverbs 13:1

"A wise son hears his father's instruction."


A truly wise son doesn't honor his father out of calculation — he recognizes that it is right to honor one's father per se, as part of the very fabric of the social order God created.

Wisdom & Understanding

David Gordon identified 40 pairings of "wisdom" and "understanding" in Scripture.


Let's read a few aloud:


  • 1 Kings 4:29
  • Job 12:12
  • Psalm 111:10
  • Proverbs 2:6
  • Colossians 1:9

These passages treat wisdom and understanding not as synonyms but as partners.

How the Wisdom Model Functions

Unlike the law model, wisdom acknowledges that different people may apply it in different ways.


Romans 14 — Observance of Days

Paul neither requires nor prohibits observing a day as religiously special. Some find Lent helpful; others don't — and both are acceptable.


The wisdom model recognizes the paradoxical and ironic nature of life.

When Wisdom Goes Unrecognized

Christians often substitute wisdom with either:


Invented Rules

Adding commands where Scripture is silent

Claims of Special Divine Guidance

Expecting God to provide personal direction for every decision


Both bypass the ordinary means God has actually appointed for these areas of life: wisdom.

Special Guidance: The Exception, Not the Rule

Two lines of evidence from Scripture:


1. Unique covenant roles

Biblical instances of special guidance typically involved kings, prophets, and priests — not ordinary believers facing everyday choices.

2. Even apostles navigated uncertainty

Jesus told the twelve: if a town won't receive you, shake the dust off your feet and move on (Matt. 10:14). No advance divine directions.

How Wisdom Guides a Specific Choice

  1. Is there a choice to be made?
  2. Does the Bible (esp. wisdom literature) address the matter generally?
  3. Does the nature of created reality commend one choice rather than another?
  4. Who are the "multitude of counselors" from whom I can learn?
  5. Are there wisdom-sayings in our language with elements of truth?
  6. Are there patterns in human history from which we can learn?
  7. Are there patterns in your life from which you can learn?
  8. Is the decision under consideration "timely"?

The Multitude of Counselors

Proverbs 15:22

"Without counsel plans fail, but with many advisers they succeed."


The fool trusts his own wisdom. The wise distrust their own opinions and solicit counsel. Seek advisers who are:

  • Diverse — who differ from you on some matters
  • Experienced — in life generally
  • Expert — in the area in question
  • Candid — both successful and willing to name their failures

Special Challenges to the Wisdom Model

Three forces in modern life actively undermine wisdom:


1. Dominance of the Law Model

Over-reliance on law neglects our God-given capacity for wisdom, making us less wise over time.

2. Technology

Constant interruption, fragmented attention, and the news cycle crowd out the reflection wisdom requires.

3. Youth Culture

Western culture idolizes youth — and in doing so, structurally devalues the wisdom that comes with age.

Technology's Three Shifts

Oral cultures

Wisdom was personal and embodied — received from real people whose character you could evaluate.

Print / literacy

The printing press severed the personal connection — you read authors whose lives you don't know.

Post-literate / digital culture

The most hostile environment for wisdom yet — phones, social media, and commercial interests systematically fragment the attention wisdom requires.

The Fast Pace of Life

Wisdom requires two things that urban, fast-paced life rarely permits:


Observation

Which takes time

Reflection

Which takes even more


Gordon's workarounds: fake reading, walking, empty headphones — disguises for solitary thought in a world uncomfortable with stillness.

Cautions About Wisdom Literature

Biblical wisdom literature (Proverbs, Ecclesiastes, Job) is rich but easily misread. Keep four characteristics in mind:


1. Memorable

Crafted to stick, not to be comprehensive

2. Practical

Aimed at real-life application

3. Partial

Individual proverbs don't cover every case

4. Paradoxical

Seemingly contradictory sayings can both be true in different contexts

Perspectives, Not Imperatives

Proverbs 14:7

"Leave the presence of a fool."


As an imperative: Would require avoiding all unbelievers — yet Jesus regularly ate with tax collectors and sinners.

As a perspective: When you need wisdom, don't expect to find it from a fool. Seek out wise people.


Genre matters. The same sentence gives wise guidance as a perspective but becomes misleading as a command.

Since Wisdom Literature Is Partial, Read the Whole

Proverbs 9:17–18

"Stolen water is sweet, and bread eaten in secret is pleasant."

(But her guests are in the depths of Sheol — v. 18)


These are the words of Woman Folly — described as knowing nothing. Missing the frame means missing the point, or worse, inverting it.

Tendencies, Not Guarantees

Proverbs 15:1 — "A soft answer turns away wrath"

Generally true — but some people are too angry to be calmed by anything. It's counsel, not a promise.

Proverbs 22:6 — "Train up a child in the way he should go"

Describes a tendency, not a guarantee. Also likely a mistranslation — "in his way" may be ironic: indulge a child's own folly, and he'll still be foolish when old.


Franklin vs. Churchill: "Early to bed, early to rise" is good general wisdom — but Churchill worked past 3 a.m. and helped win WWII.

The Commend/Command Distinction

One of the great benefits of the wisdom model: it lets us commend what we cannot command.


Law Model

Permits commanding what God has explicitly revealed — don't steal, don't murder.

Wisdom Model

Allows offering personal counsel in areas Scripture doesn't address — without claiming a "thus saith the Lord" we don't have.

Commend/Command in Practice

Evangelist Leighton Ford recommends reading five psalms and a chapter of Proverbs daily — working through both books monthly.


Gordon has found this practice genuinely helpful — but neither man would ever command it. They commend it from experience, and leave the decision where it belongs: with the individual.


A church needn't have a position on extrabiblical matters — but its officers, as experienced people, should feel free to offer wise counsel personally.

Key Takeaways

1. Many of life's most important decisions fall outside specific biblical commands — they require wisdom, not just rule-following

2. Wisdom is cultivated slowly through fear of the Lord, humble observation, sustained reflection, and a multitude of counselors

3. Wisdom literature must be read as perspectives and tendencies — not absolute promises or legal guarantees

4. Three forces undermine wisdom today: dominance of law model, technology, and youth culture

5. The commend/command distinction lets us offer wise counsel where Scripture is silent — without overclaiming divine authority
