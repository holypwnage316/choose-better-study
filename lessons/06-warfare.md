# The Warfare Model

[Back to Home](../)

## Key Question

*In the often invisible, yet real warfare between the forces of good and evil, will this decision likely serve the forces of good or the forces of evil?*

## Overview

Beneath everything else recorded in biblical history is the great warfare between Satan, God's rebellious creature, and God himself. Satan is malevolent, attempting to destroy all true pleasure, health, happiness, and holiness. God is benevolent, ultimately establishing in his created order the richest pleasure, health, happiness, and holiness. As early as Genesis 3, even as God cursed the created order because of Adam's sin, he promised that there would be a great war in which his purposes would triumph (Gen. 3:14–15). The word translated "bruise" there is rare — in Job 9:17 it is translated "crush," which may be more indicative of a serious wound than "bruise."

One of the most common titles for God in the Bible, used 259 times, is *Lord Sabaoth* — the Lord of Hosts, or the Lord of Armies. The title refers to God as he wages terrifying (and ultimately triumphant) war against his enemies and the enemies of his people. As a benevolent sovereign, he will not permit those who revolt against his reign to destroy the inhabitants of his realm, and he therefore wages warfare against the revolutionaries. While much of God's activity as Divine Warrior occurs in the context of geopolitical Israel, this is nothing less than a type of his eschatological warfare, anticipated in Genesis 3 and consummated in the realities recorded in the book of Revelation.

> **Discussion:** One of God's most common titles in Scripture is *Lord Sabaoth* — Lord of Armies. Does thinking of God primarily as a warrior feel natural or uncomfortable to you? How does it change how you think about the Christian life?

## The Biblical Basis for the Warfare Model

The warfare model draws from three streams of Scripture: 

(1) Old Testament typology, where expectations of Christ often take the form of a military king leading God's people to victory; 

(2) apocalyptic passages that frame the whole drama of human history as a war between good and evil, light and darkness; and 

(3) the many New Testament passages that use military language to describe the Christian life — our weapons, our armor, our enemies, our internal conflicts:

> For though we walk in the flesh, we are not waging war according to the flesh. For the weapons of our warfare are not of the flesh but have divine power to destroy strongholds. (2 Cor. 10:3–4)

> Put on the whole armor of God, that you may be able to stand against the schemes of the devil. For we do not wrestle against flesh and blood, but against the rulers, against the authorities, against the cosmic powers over this present darkness, against the spiritual forces of evil in the heavenly places. Therefore take up the whole armor of God, that you may be able to withstand in the evil day, and having done all, to stand firm. (Eph. 6:11–13)

> What causes quarrels and what causes fights among you? Is it not this, that your passions are at war within you? (James 4:1)

> Beloved, I urge you as sojourners and exiles to abstain from the passions of the flesh, which wage war against your soul. (1 Peter 2:11)

### Apocalyptic Warfare in the Bible

Scripture's vision of the end of history is consistently military. God appears as a conquering warrior who defeats the enemies of his people and restores order, justice, and peace (Rev. 19:11–21). The conflict is not only earthly — there is warfare in heaven itself, with Michael and his angels fighting the dragon (Rev. 12:7), and the beast waging war against the Lamb (Rev. 17). Even within the churches, Christ threatens war against those who embrace false teaching (Rev. 2). Taken together, these visions make clear that there is no neutrality — righteousness and wickedness, submission to God and resistance to his reign, are utterly incompatible.

### Military Descriptions of the Christian Ministry and the Christian Life

The New Testament consistently frames both Christian ministry and the Christian life in military terms. Paul calls his fellow ministers soldiers (Phil. 2:25; 2 Tim. 2:3–4; Philemon 1:2) and charges Timothy to "wage the good warfare" (1 Tim. 1:18). Jesus himself describes the church as a militant force breaking open the prison walls of Hades (Matt. 16:18) — an image of assault, not defense.

The language runs deeper than metaphor. The word Paul uses for "instruments" in Romans 6:13 (*hopla*) is standard military vocabulary for weapons or armor — the same word used in John 18:3, Romans 13:12, and 2 Corinthians 10:4. Similarly, the word translated "are opposed to" in Galatians 5:17 (flesh vs. Spirit) is a martial term for battlefield opponents — the same word used for the devil himself in 1 Timothy 5:14.

The church has long recognized this. Ancient baptismal rites — found in Tertullian, Hippolytus, Cyril of Jerusalem, and still present in the 1928 Book of Common Prayer — included a vow to *renounce the devil and all his works*. The implication is that baptism is an enlistment: the convert leaves the life of a citizen and begins the life of a soldier.

This warfare is also described as mutual *resistance*. Satan and the fallen structures of human society resist God's purposes; God's people are called to resist the resistance. Romans 12:2 — "Do not be conformed to this world" — captures this exactly. The world is hostile to God's created purposes, and to follow Christ means refusing to be molded by that hostility.

## How the Warfare Model Functions

A crucial caveat before examining how this model works: none of it is technique. Progress in spiritual warfare depends entirely on Christ — he alone has defeated the Serpent, and every smaller victory believers experience is an extension of his work. We engage this warfare the same way a preacher delivers a sermon: wholly dependent on the Spirit.

### Watchfulness

Armies cannot afford to be caught off guard — which is why standing watch has always been a basic military necessity. Scripture gives the same command to believers, warning that the devil actively hunts for the unprepared (1 Peter 5:8).

### Obedience to Orders

Obedience is a universal feature of military life. The battlefield is not the place for debate — orders are followed. Jesus marveled at the centurion who understood this and applied the same logic to faith:

> But the centurion replied, "Lord, I am not worthy to have you come under my roof, but only say the word, and my servant will be healed. For I too am a man under authority, with soldiers under me. And I say to one, 'Go,' and he goes, and to another, 'Come,' and he comes, and to my servant, 'Do this,' and he does it." When Jesus heard this, he marveled and said to those who followed him, "Truly, I tell you, with no one in Israel have I found such faith." (Matt. 8:8–10)

The warfare model therefore includes everything the law model includes: soldiers must obey.

### Equipment and Preparation

Equipment determines outcomes — American industrial capacity after Pearl Harbor is a prime example: the speed of the Pacific Fleet's rebuild surprised Japan and ultimately overwhelmed the Axis. The cost of equipping a single soldier rose from $170 in WWII to over $17,000 in recent conflicts, reflecting how decisive proper preparation has become. An ill-equipped army loses regardless of courage. Scripture calls believers to the same seriousness: the armor of God (Eph. 6) and weapons with divine power (2 Cor. 10:3–6).

### Strategy — Offensive and Defensive

Military strategy operates on two fronts. Offensively, commanders determine where they can achieve the greatest victories; defensively, they identify their own weaknesses and work to protect them. The key questions are: where would a victory be most significant? Where would a defeat be most costly? Where is the enemy weak? Where am I weak? Believers must think in these same terms — developing plans to take ground not currently ours, while honestly assessing personal, ecclesiastical, and cultural vulnerabilities the Enemy might exploit.

### Knowledge of the Enemy

> What I have forgiven, if I have forgiven anything, has been for your sake in the presence of Christ, so that we would not be outwitted by Satan; for we are not ignorant of his designs. (2 Cor. 2:10–11)

Today, most of us are ignorant of his designs. Could you name five of them? We know Satan tries to break our communion with God and tempt us to sin — but do we know *how* he goes about either? Consider our high-tech culture: what Satan desires most is to disrupt our communion with God, and a world of constant social media, texting, and notifications makes uninterrupted prayer and meditation nearly impossible. We must understand how the Enemy uses the tools of our moment so that we can use those tools deliberately rather than letting them use us.

## Special Challenges to the Warfare Model

### Satan's Weapons: Deceit and Desire

Scripture consistently reveals two primary weapons in Satan's arsenal: deceit and desire (especially strong desire, or passion).

**Deceit.** Satan shapes how we perceive reality, and we behave according to that perception. Jesus said of him, "He was a murderer from the beginning, and does not stand in the truth, because there is no truth in him. When he lies, he speaks out of his own character, for he is a liar and the father of lies" (John 8:44). His original deception — convincing Adam and Eve that God's purposes were malicious and that following him was wiser than obeying God — remains his primary strategy today. He always attempts to make God appear malevolent and obedience appear ruinous. The truth is the opposite: following Satan debases and dehumanizes, substituting fleeting pleasures for lasting ones. As Proverbs 14:12 warns, "There is a way that seems right to a man, but its end is the way to death." Thomas Brooks called this Satan's great device: to present the bait and hide the hook.

Perhaps the most effective instrument of deceit is cultural habit. Every culture establishes what is "normal" — shaming some values and honoring others, making some ideas more plausible than others. Since all cultures are infected by human rebellion, what a culture treats as normal may be far from what God reveals as true. We should ask: does our culture's preference for youth over age accord more with the fifth commandment? Does our consumerism accord with the teaching of Jesus? Does our love of novelty accord with Scripture's reverence for the faith of past saints (Heb. 11)?

> **Discussion:** Gordon says our culture treats certain things as "normal" that may not accord with God's revealed norms — he uses our preference for youth over age, consumerism, and love of novelty as examples. What other cultural assumptions might we have absorbed without realizing they conflict with Scripture?

*Diversion* is a special form of deception. When Satan cannot convince us that good is evil, he diverts our energy away from what matters. He tried this with Christ in Matthew 4 — unable to destroy Christ's power, he attempted to redirect it toward meaningless displays. The church faces the same temptation: the Enemy routinely diverts her from her principal task — the spiritual and eternal wellbeing of her people — toward social reform movements, petty theological squabbling, and internal litigation. He would rather the church fight herself than fight him. He takes delight in pulling ministers away from the proclamation of God's word, and in cultivating congregational appetite for entertainment over the means of grace: prayer, the Word, and the sacraments.

**Desires.** Beyond deceit, Satan also exploits our corrupt desires. Because of the fall, our desires are upside-down — we want the creation more than the Creator. These desires can grow so strong that the Puritans called them "violent," and at their most intense, passions. When passions take hold, reason goes out the window, and apart from God's grace they can feel completely irresistible.

> What causes quarrels and what causes fights among you? Is it not this, that your passions are at war within you? You desire and do not have, so you murder. You covet and cannot obtain, so you fight and quarrel. (James 4:1–2)

> Beloved, I urge you as sojourners and exiles to abstain from the passions of the flesh, which wage war against your soul. (1 Peter 2:11)

### Our Weapons: Faith and Repentance

Faith and repentance are the direct antidotes to Satan's two weapons — faith counters deceit, repentance counters desire. They appear together throughout the New Testament because they are the two sides of the same response to the gospel (Mark 1:14–15; Acts 20:20–21; Heb. 6:1).

Faith assents to and rests in what God reveals. The more a believer's mind is shaped by Scripture — through study, reflection, and instruction — the harder it is for the Enemy to deceive. Ignorant and uncertain faith is easily manipulated; thoughtful, well-grounded faith is far more resistant. True faith is not mere intellectual assent but a heartfelt trust in God's revelation, resting on the conviction that God is loving and wise.

Repentance is the antidote to sinful desire — the deliberate, intentional determination to exercise self-control and self-denial. True repentance is not mere resolve; it is a resolve grounded in a genuine, soul-felt recognition of how vile and deadly sin is.

Faith and repentance are like a soldier's two legs: with one we stride toward God, with the other we stride away from sin.

## Application of the Warfare Model

The warfare model raises the stakes of decision-making: every decision either strengthens or weakens us for the next battle. It pushes us to look beyond immediate consequences to longer-range ones, and to think strategically — both offensively (what ground can I take that I don't currently hold?) and defensively (what hard-fought ground do I need to protect?).

Consider Mary and Bill, both members of the same church. Mary is a busy wife and mother involved in many activities — her faith is sincere, but her schedule leaves little room for prayer, study, or meditation. Bill is single and contemplative, but somewhat withdrawn from others. When their church asks for someone to chair a new ministry, the warfare model suggests different answers for each. Mary is already overextended and needs more contemplative space, not more activity — for her, saying no is the strategic choice. Bill, on the other hand, needs precisely this kind of active service to develop areas where he is underdeveloped — for him, saying yes takes new ground from the Enemy. Same question, two legitimate answers. The warfare model illuminates what other models alone might not.

The same logic applies to choosing a local church. Church membership has strategic consequences for both the family and the congregation. At some points in life, joining a smaller struggling church or church plant will demand more of us but do more for the kingdom; at other points, the opposite may be true. Two families thinking strategically might reach different conclusions — and both could be right.

These examples also show how the five models work together. A question that is strategic is also a question of wisdom, and it affects communion with God. While any lawful ministry in any legitimate church is an occasion to imitate God, not every decision carries the same weight — for our wisdom, our communion, or the warfare between the seed of the Serpent and the Seed of the woman.

## Key Takeaways

- The Christian life involves real spiritual conflict
- Our choices have kingdom significance
- Victory is assured in Christ, but vigilance is required
