# The Communion Model

[Back to Home](../) | [Slides](05-communion-slides.html)

!! DON'T FORGET TO PRAY *(Slides 1–2)*

## Key Question *(Slide 3)*

*How might this decision enhance or inhibit my (or our) communion with God?*

## Overview *(Slide 4)*

The Communion Model understands human life at its core as a life of ongoing fellowship with God. Unlike the other models which start with law, wisdom, or consequences, this one starts with a simple but profound claim: you were made *for* relationship with God — and that relationship is not a component of your life, it *is* your life.

Augustine said it plainly: "Thou hast made us for thyself, and restless is our heart until it comes to rest in thee." That restlessness isn't a bug — it's a built-in signal pointing us back to God. Pascal picked up on this too when he observed that most human misery comes from our inability to simply sit quietly before God. We busy ourselves, distract ourselves, and fill our lives with noise — and in doing so, we drift from the very thing we were made for.

John Owen gave us a helpful framework here. He said communion with God is a genuine two-way exchange — God communicates himself to us, and we return to him. And he was honest that this communion has two stages: here and now, we experience it partially — like the first light of dawn, real but incomplete. In eternity, we'll experience it fully. So in this life, our "rest" in God always carries an element of "unrest" — we taste it, but we're not home yet.

This shapes how the model works ethically. Every decision you make in life is a fork in the road. One path moves you *toward* God — it deepens your awareness of him, your dependence on him, your enjoyment of him. The other path moves you *away* — not necessarily into outright sin, but into distraction, self-sufficiency, or numbness to his presence. The communion model asks you to pay attention to that fork.

For the communion model, prayer is not something one does every now and then or in seasons of special need. Life itself is a prayer and fellowship with God. So the guiding question isn't just "Is this wrong?" — it's "Does this draw me closer to God, or does it pull me away?"

> **Discussion:** Augustine said our hearts are restless until they rest in God. Do you think most people today would recognize that restlessness in themselves — or do they tend to explain it some other way? *(Slide 5)*

## Biblical Basis for the Communion Model *(Slide 6)*

The communion model isn't just built on humanity's *ability* to communicate with God — it's built on the reality that God actively addresses us and teaches us to address him in return. Even after the fall, when our first parents were banished from God's fellowship in the garden, God didn't go silent. He continued to speak through prophets and through Scripture — and much of that Scripture includes the requirement that his people pray to him.

This is why Israel's life with God is recorded not only in the historical books but also in the Psalms — an entire collection of devotional literature that preserves the ongoing communication of God's people with him: thanksgiving, lament, praise, and more.

It's worth noting that Adam and Eve's sin was fundamentally a communion problem. They chose to listen to the voice of the creature (the Serpent) rather than the voice of the Creator. Paul captures the tragic pattern in Romans 1: people "exchanged the glory of the immortal God" and "worshiped and served the creature rather than the Creator" (Rom. 1:21–25). Sin, at its root, is a disordering of our communion — turning *from* God *toward* something else.

The remedy, then, is a reordering of that communion. Paul calls redeemed people to "pray without ceasing," giving thanks *always* and *for everything* to God the Father in the name of Christ (Eph. 5:20; 6:18; 1 Thess. 5:16–18). The Protestant Reformers captured this with the phrase *coram Deo* — living "in the presence of God." As Paul writes in Romans 14:7–8: "None of us lives to himself, and none of us dies to himself. For if we live, we live *to the Lord*, and if we die, we die *to the Lord*." This passage became one of the prooftexts for the famous first question and answer of the Heidelberg Catechism.

What follows is a brief overview of some of the biblical evidence that suggests that life itself may be thought of as communion with God and that therefore any attempt to evaluate life (the ethical task) must consider whether a given choice contributes to, or detracts from, this basic purpose.

### Genesis 1–3; 11; 28 *(Slide 7)*

The creation account depicts humans as distinctly made in God's image — which means, among other things, that we are capable of communicating with God. Some theologians actually define the "image of God" primarily as this capacity to relate to him. God speaks to Adam and Eve in the garden, and even after they revolt against his will and he banishes them, he continues to address them — with words of both judgment and promised redemption (Gen. 3:16–19).

Communion with God is greatly disrupted by this curse-banishment, and it will not be fully reestablished until the end of the age when we shall see God face to face (1 John 3:2). Yet communion continues, even now, in a damaged and degraded form — because to sever it entirely would be to destroy human life as such. Paul hints at this when he writes to Timothy:

> She who is truly a widow, left all alone, has *set her hope on God* and *continues in supplications and prayers night and day*, but she who is self-indulgent is *dead even while she lives*. (1 Tim. 5:5–6)

The curse-banishment is a harsh reality for those made in God's image. But Revelation 21:3–4 promises its ultimate reversal:

> And I heard a loud voice from the throne saying, "Behold, the dwelling place of God is with man. He will dwell with them, and they will be his people, and God himself will be with them as their God. He will wipe away every tear from their eyes, and death shall be no more, neither shall there be mourning, nor crying, nor pain anymore, for the former things have passed away."

When God reverses the banishment and dwells with humans again, he reverses the curses as well — no more mourning, tears, or dying. In between, we suffer from the painful tension: our created nature longs for communion with God, yet we are banished from the fullness of his presence.

Genesis 11 and 28 show two sides of this longing. At Babel, rebellious humanity tries to "storm heaven" by building a tower up to where God reigns — a story that ends in divine judgment. But in Genesis 28, what they grasped after is *given* to Jacob in a dream: a ladder between heaven and earth, a highway for angelic beings ascending and descending, renewing communion between heaven and earth. It's a foretaste of Revelation 21. Until Jacob's dream becomes reality, all our communion with God is impoverished and partial.

This longing — for the moment when "the dwelling place of God will be with men" — is what C. S. Lewis called *Sehnsucht*: the inconsolable longing for "we know not what." But at some level we do know. It is our banishment from full communion with God. No idol can satisfy it. Only the next life will fulfill it completely. But even now, in this era of curse-banishment, God permits us to meet him by the special means he has appointed — and he still addresses rebellious humans and allows us to address him.

### God Communicates to Us *(Slide 9)*

In the pattern of biblical communion, God addresses us first, and we reply to his address. He does this in three ways: through his creation, through his Word and sacraments, and most supremely, through his incarnate Son.

God communicates through creation — what theologians call "natural" or "general" revelation, because what God has made in the natural realm reveals something about his nature to everyone. As Paul writes, "His invisible attributes, namely, his eternal power and divine nature, have been clearly perceived, ever since the creation of the world, in the things that have been made" (Rom. 1:20). The psalmist celebrated the same reality: "The heavens declare the glory of God, and the sky above proclaims his handiwork" (Ps. 19:1).

God has also spoken specially and verbally to his covenant people through his Word and sacraments — what theologians call "special revelation." Paul notes that the great privilege of the Jewish people was that "they were entrusted with the oracles of God" (Rom. 3:1–2). Moses himself marveled at this:

> For what great nation is there that has a god so near to it as the LORD our God is to us, whenever we call upon him? And what great nation is there, that has statutes and rules so righteous as all this law that I set before you today? (Deut. 4:7–8)

To Israel, through circumcision and Passover; to the church, through baptism and the Lord's Supper — God has manifested himself to his people in a special way.

But God's supreme disclosure came when the eternal Son became incarnate. As the author of Hebrews writes:

> Long ago, at many times and in many ways, God spoke to our fathers by the prophets, but in these last days he has spoken to us by his Son, whom he appointed the heir of all things, through whom also he created the world. He is the radiance of the glory of God and the exact imprint of his nature, and he upholds the universe by the word of his power. After making purification for sins, he sat down at the right hand of the Majesty on high, having become as much superior to angels as the name he has inherited is more excellent than theirs. (Heb. 1:1–4)

This is why John called Christ the "Word" of God:

> In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God. He was in the beginning with God. All things were made through him, and without him was not any thing made that was made. . . . And the Word became flesh and dwelt among us, and we have seen his glory, glory as of the only Son from the Father, full of grace and truth. (John 1:1–3, 14)

Jesus himself confirmed this: "Whoever has seen me has seen the Father" (John 14:9). The fullest revelation of God's character is manifested through the incarnation.

### We Reply to God *(Slides 10–10b)*

When God addresses humans through his creation or through his Word and sacrament, he calls those same humans to reply to him. Many biblical texts call us to verbally communicate with God — to praise, thank, and sing to him. A few key passages show what this looks like:

*"Praying at all times in the Spirit, with all prayer and supplication"* (Eph. 6:18). The original Greek reads "pray in every *season* in the Spirit." Paul's point is not that prayer can never be interrupted, but that we are to pray in every season and on every occasion of life. Joyous seasons, sad seasons, frustrating seasons — all of them are appropriate seasons for prayer, because human life is designed to be a life of fellowship with our Maker.

*"Rejoice always, pray without ceasing, give thanks in all circumstances; for this is the will of God in Christ Jesus for you"* (1 Thess. 5:16–18). Again, the point is not that one can never pause from praying. The point is that no event or circumstance — neither joy nor sorrow, pain nor pleasure — should be permitted to *interrupt* prayer or cause it to stop. God's will for the creature made in his image is for that creature to learn to commune with him in life's varying moments.

*"Is anyone among you suffering? Let him pray. Is anyone cheerful? Let him sing praise"* (James 5:13). Whether suffering or cheerful, one communes with God — suffering should lead us to prayer, and cheerfulness should lead us to praise. Much of the challenge of the Christian pilgrimage consists in learning to do both. Some find it easier to commune with God when life is difficult, others when life is going well. For all of us, the goal is to experience either suffering or cheerfulness *in communion with God*.

### The Psalms *(Slide 11)*

The entire Psalter testifies to a life in communion with God. Throughout the Psalms, Israel rejoices, thanks, laments, celebrates, and mourns — whatever life consists of, both pain and joy, she communicates it to God. Strikingly, over seventy of the one hundred fifty psalms are laments, making lament the most common category. This proves that God not only permits but invites us to commune with him when life is challenging, frightening, sorrowful, or perplexing.

This stands in sharp contrast to the religions surrounding Israel. Ancient Near Eastern and Greek religions were largely prosperity-based — when life went well, the gods were pleased; when life went badly, religion went on hold. There was no ordinary place for piety or communion with the deity in suffering. Biblical religion challenges this entirely. Suffering does not disprove that we are God's people, nor does it put communion on hold. We remain reconciled to God through the work of Christ, and he promises to sustain us in our neediest moments.

Lament also challenges us to commune *with* those who suffer. When Israel gathered for worship, the entire congregation chanted lament psalms together — even those who weren't personally experiencing the suffering described. The non-sufferer identified sympathetically with the sufferer, letting the sufferer's pain become his own, at least imaginatively. His voice joined the sufferer's, breaking through the loneliness that so often accompanies pain. An Israelite never suffered alone — the covenant community lamented with him, and together they entered the presence of God, who welcomes the afflicted.

> **Discussion:** Over half the Psalms are laments. Was that surprising to you? How does that change how you think about bringing pain, confusion, or frustration to God — and which of the modes of communing (praise, lament, thanksgiving, supplication) do you think gets the least attention in the church today? *(Slide 12)*

### A Good Life, Unlike a Wicked Life, Is One That Is “Close to,” “Near to,” or “with” God *(Slide 13)*

Scripture consistently describes righteous life using spatial language — walking *with* God, walking *before* God, being *near* to God. Noah “walked with God” (Gen. 6:9). God called Abram to “walk before me, and be blameless” (Gen. 17:1). Jesus promised that those who love him will experience the Father and Son coming to make their *home* with them (John 14:23). And Paul declares that those once “far off have been brought near by the blood of Christ” (Eph. 2:13).

The flipside is equally telling. Isaiah rebukes those who “draw near with their mouth and honor me with their lips, while their hearts are far from me” (Isa. 29:13). The wicked are those who hide their deeds from God, who say, “Who sees us? Who knows us?” — as if life could be lived outside God's presence.

Paul ties it together in Romans 3:11, echoing Psalms 14 and 15: “No one understands; no one seeks for God.” For Paul, the fact that no one *seeks* God is undeniable evidence of human corruption. To make sense of life — or to accept life — without seeking communion with one's Creator is the very essence of fallenness and rebellion.

### Psalm 73 as a Test Case *(Slides 14–15)*

Psalm 73 is a lament psalm that puts the communion model on display. The psalmist begins candidly — he nearly lost his footing because he envied the prosperity of the wicked while he himself suffered:

> Truly God is good to Israel,
>       to those who are pure in heart.
> But as for me, my feet had almost stumbled,
>       my steps had nearly slipped.
> For I was envious of the arrogant
>       when I saw the prosperity of the wicked. (vv. 1–3)

After unpacking his complaint through verse 14, the turning point arrives in verses 15–18:

> If I had said, "I will speak thus,"
>       I would have betrayed the generation of your children.
> But when I thought how to understand this,
>       it seemed to me a wearisome task,
> until I went into the sanctuary of God;
>       then I discerned their end.
> Truly you set them in slippery places;
>       you make them fall to ruin.

The turning point was communion. He would have *betrayed* God's people had he merely kept complaining *about* God and his providence. But when he went into the sanctuary — when he communed *with* God rather than complaining *about* him — his mind changed. He then resolves:

> When my soul was embittered,
>       when I was pricked in heart,
> I was brutish and ignorant;
>       I was like a beast toward you.
> Nevertheless, I am continually with you;
>       you hold my right hand.
> You guide me with your counsel,
>       and afterward you will receive me to glory.
> Whom have I in heaven but you?
>       And there is nothing on earth that I desire besides you.
> My flesh and my heart may fail,
>       but God is the strength of my heart and my portion forever.
> For behold, those who are far from you shall perish;
>       you put an end to everyone who is unfaithful to you.
> But for me it is good to be near God;
>       I have made the Lord GOD my refuge,
>       that I may tell of all your works. (vv. 21–28)

The psalmist calls himself "brutish" and "like a beast" — significant language, since it is communication and discourse that distinguish humans from animals. When he stopped communing with God, he became less than human. The climax of the psalm — one of the great understatements in all of Scripture — is verse 28: *"But for me it is good to be near God."*

Note the contrast in verses 27–28: those who are "far from" God perish; those who are "near God" flourish. All sin, therefore, is a turning away from God:

- We turn away from his praise and approval to seek the praise and approval of men.
- We turn away from his counsel to seek the counsel of men.
- We turn away from his comfort to find comfort in what God made.
- We turn away from his promises to attempt to secure our own wellbeing.
- We turn away from the noble pleasures he approves to pursue ignoble pleasures that he disapproves.

Any Christian ethical reasoning must wrestle with the reality that human life is designed to be a life of communion with God. Therefore, to properly evaluate life and its choices, we must consider communion. Do our choices foster communion with God, or do they detract from it?

## How the Communion Model Functions *(Slides 17–18)*

The communion model asks a practical question about every decision: How will the various options likely affect individual or corporate communion with God? This includes considering what practices, environments, relationships, and activities have enhanced communion with God in the past — and how the choice under consideration fits with what Scripture and Christian tradition have said about life with God.

Sometimes the answers are fairly straightforward. Consider a job change: if the current location has a strong, edifying church community and the new location has none, communion with God will likely suffer if the family moves. On the other hand, someone with a gift for church-planting might deliberately move to the underserved area precisely to build that community. In both cases, the communion model is operating — the question is how the decision will affect life with God, individually and corporately.

Other questions are less obvious but no less important.

#### Work

If prayer and meditation require time, energy, and quiet, what is the likely effect of a job that demands seventy to eighty hours a week? Much attention has been paid to the physical and familial costs of overwork. Fewer have observed that the same realities diminish communion with God.

#### Leisure

How we spend our free time either contributes to or detracts from a life of communion with God. Certain activities open the mind and soul to spiritual reflection; others close it down. The answers here are somewhat relative — what cultivates communion for one person may not for another. But each of us should be able to identify leisure activities that tend to enhance our communion with God and those that tend to diminish it.

#### Home Environment

What kind of home promotes communion with God — one filled with constant noise, screens, and distraction, or one with regular quiet and space for reflection? The question, once raised, tends to answer itself.

#### Technology

Communion with God requires some degree of uninterrupted silence. In Pascal's day, the problem was the lack of *desire* to sit in a quiet room alone. In our day, the problem is the near impossibility of *finding* such a room. Constant notifications, alarms, and devices that travel with us make sustained prayer and meditation increasingly difficult. Cultivating an environment that permits quiet — making deliberate choices about our use of technology — is becoming an increasingly important dimension of the ethical task.

> **Discussion:** In contemporary Western culture, few have a natural disposition toward contemplation and stillness. What are some of the specific challenges you face in creating a truly quiet environment? What decisions are you currently considering where the communion model might help you think differently? *(Slide 19)*

## Special Challenges to the Communion Model *(Slide 20)*

One particular challenge of the communion model is **subjectivity**. While some devotional practices have been found beneficial across the ages — prayer, Bible reading, corporate worship — we are probably not very good judges of our own souls. We have to experiment to discover which practices most effectively cultivate communion with God for us personally. What opens one person's heart to God may do little for another.

A second challenge is our **cultural moment**. In Pascal's seventeenth-century world, a quiet room was at least available to those who sought one, even if few sought it. In our moment, noise is nearly ubiquitous. Finding the quiet that communion with God requires often demands deliberate, countercultural choices about how we structure our time, our homes, and our relationship with technology.

## Key Takeaways *(Slide 21)*

- Human life is designed to be a life of communion with God
- God initiates — he speaks first through creation, Scripture, and his Son; we reply through prayer, praise, and lament
- Sin is fundamentally a turning *away* from God; righteousness is a turning *toward* him
- Every decision is a fork in the road — one path moves us closer to God, the other away
- The goal is not rule-keeping but closeness — *"it is good to be near God"* (Ps. 73:28)
