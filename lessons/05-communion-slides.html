Lesson 5: Life as Fellowship with God

Opening Prayer

Let us begin with prayer

Key Question


How might this decision enhance or inhibit my (or our) communion with God?


Unlike other models which start with law, wisdom, or consequences, this one starts with a simple but profound claim:


You were made for relationship with God — and that relationship is not a component of your life, it is your life.

Life as Communion

"Thou hast made us for thyself, and restless is our heart until it comes to rest in thee."

— Augustine

John Owen: Communion is a genuine two-way exchange — God communicates himself to us, and we return to him. It has two stages:

Now

Initial, incomplete communion — like the first light of dawn, real but partial

Eternity

Perfect, complete communion — the fullness of his presence

Discussion Question

Augustine said our hearts are restless until they rest in God.

Do you think most people today would recognize that restlessness in themselves — or do they tend to explain it some other way?

Biblical Basis for the Communion Model

The model rests not just on humanity's ability to communicate with God, but on the reality that God actively addresses us and teaches us to address him in return.

Even after the fall, God didn't go silent. He continued to speak through prophets and Scripture — and much of Scripture includes the requirement that his people pray to him.

"None of us lives to himself, and none of us dies to himself. For if we live, we live to the Lord, and if we die, we die to the Lord."

— Romans 14:7–8

Genesis 1–3; 11; 28

Humans are distinctly made in God's image — which includes the capacity to communicate with God. Even after the fall and banishment, communion continues in a damaged form.

Revelation 21:3–4

"Behold, the dwelling place of God is with man. He will dwell with them... He will wipe away every tear from their eyes, and death shall be no more."

Genesis 11 (Babel) shows humanity grasping for God's presence; Genesis 28 (Jacob's ladder) shows God giving what they grasped after — a foretaste of Revelation 21.

Discussion Question

Adam and Eve's sin was essentially a communion failure — they listened to the creature rather than the Creator.

Where do you see that same pattern playing out in everyday life today?

God Communicates to Us

God addresses us first. He does this in three ways:

1. Creation (Natural Revelation)

"His invisible attributes... have been clearly perceived... in the things that have been made." (Rom. 1:20)

2. Word & Sacraments (Special Revelation)

"The Jews were entrusted with the oracles of God." (Rom. 3:1–2)

3. The Incarnate Son (Supreme Disclosure)

"Whoever has seen me has seen the Father." (John 14:9)

We Reply to God

When God addresses us, he calls us to reply — in every season and circumstance of life.


Ephesians 6:18

"Praying at all times in the Spirit, with all prayer and supplication." — Pray in every season, not just moments of crisis.

1 Thessalonians 5:16–18

"Rejoice always, pray without ceasing, give thanks in all circumstances; for this is the will of God in Christ Jesus for you."

We Reply to God

No circumstance — joy or sorrow, pain or pleasure — should be permitted to interrupt prayer or cause it to stop.


James 5:13

"Is anyone among you suffering? Let him pray. Is anyone cheerful? Let him sing praise."


Whether suffering or cheerful — the goal is to experience every season in communion with God.

The Psalms

The entire Psalter testifies to a life in communion with God — whatever life consists of, Israel communicates it to God.


Over 70 of 150 psalms are laments — the most common category. God not only permits but invites us to commune with him when life is challenging, frightening, or perplexing.


This stands in sharp contrast to ancient Near Eastern religions, where suffering meant divine displeasure and put religion on hold. Biblical religion challenges this entirely — suffering does not disrupt communion.

Discussion Question

Over half the Psalms are laments. Was that surprising to you?

How does that change how you think about bringing pain, confusion, or frustration to God — and which mode of communing (praise, lament, thanksgiving, supplication) do you think gets the least attention in the church today?

A Good Life Is One "Near" God

Scripture consistently describes righteous life using spatial language:

  • Noah "walked with God" (Gen. 6:9)
  • God called Abram to "walk before me, and be blameless" (Gen. 17:1)
  • Those once "far off have been brought near by the blood of Christ" (Eph. 2:13)

Isaiah rebukes those who "draw near with their mouth... while their hearts are far from me." (Isa. 29:13)

Paul: "No one seeks for God" (Rom. 3:11) — the very essence of fallenness is a failure to seek communion.

Psalm 73 as a Test Case

The psalmist nearly lost his footing envying the prosperity of the wicked — until the turning point:

"...until I went into the sanctuary of God; then I discerned their end." (v. 17)

The turning point was communion. He communed with God rather than complaining about him — and his mind changed.

"But for me it is good to be near God; I have made the Lord GOD my refuge." (v. 28)

All Sin Is a Turning Away

Note the contrast in Psalm 73:27–28 — those "far from" God perish; those "near God" flourish.


  • We turn away from his praise to seek the praise of men
  • We turn away from his counsel to seek the counsel of men
  • We turn away from his comfort to find comfort in creation
  • We turn away from his promises to secure our own wellbeing
  • We turn away from noble pleasures he approves to pursue ignoble ones

Discussion Question

How does the communion model conflict with a legalistic approach to the Christian life?

What's the difference between following rules to stay out of trouble and making choices to stay close to God?

How the Communion Model Functions

The model asks a practical question about every decision:

How will the various options likely affect individual or corporate communion with God?

This includes considering past practices that have enhanced communion, what Scripture and Christian tradition say, and what we know about our own particular nature.

Example: A Job Change

If the current location has a strong, edifying church community and the new location has none — communion with God will likely suffer if the family moves.

Applying the Model

Work

A 70–80 hour work week leaves little time for prayer, meditation, or reflection. Few observe that overwork diminishes communion with God.

Leisure

Our free time either contributes to or detracts from communion. Each of us should be able to name activities that draw us closer — and those that don't.

Home & Technology

Pascal: the problem in his day was the lack of desire for a quiet room. In our day, it's the near impossibility of finding one.

Discussion Question

In contemporary Western culture, few have a natural disposition toward contemplation and stillness.

What are some of the specific challenges you face in creating a truly quiet environment? What decisions are you currently considering where the communion model might help you think differently?

Special Challenges to the Communion Model

Subjectivity

Some practices are universally beneficial (prayer, Bible reading, corporate worship), but we are probably not very good judges of our own souls. We must experiment to discover what most effectively cultivates communion for us personally.

Our Cultural Moment

Noise is nearly ubiquitous. Finding the quiet that communion requires often demands deliberate, countercultural choices about our time, our homes, and our relationship with technology.

Key Takeaways

1. Human life is designed to be a life of communion with God

2. God initiates — he speaks through creation, Scripture, and his Son; we reply through prayer, praise, and lament

3. Sin is fundamentally a turning away from God; righteousness is a turning toward him

4. Every decision is a fork in the road — one path moves us closer to God, the other away

5. The goal is not rule-keeping but closeness — "it is good to be near God" (Ps. 73:28)
